Know Your Enemy: Types of Phishing

Phishing

Broad attacks sent to a massive number of people, hoping a few will bite. Often impersonates large companies like banks or tech giants.

Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks aimed at specific individuals or companies. The emails are personalized with names, job titles, or other details to appear more legitimate.

Whaling / BEC

Highly targeted attacks aimed at senior executives ("whales") or involving impersonation of a CEO to trick employees into making wire transfers.

A Hierarchy of Deception

Not all phishing attacks are created equal. They range from wide-net, generic scams to highly sophisticated and personalized attacks aimed at high-value targets. The more targeted the attack, the harder it can be to spot.

Understanding this hierarchy helps you appreciate the different levels of threat you might face, from a random fake password reset email to a carefully crafted message that seems to come directly from your boss.

Before you click any link or open any attachment, take five seconds to look for these common red flags. This chart shows the most common signs of a phishing attempt.

Examples of Red Flags in Action:

This image shows a real phishing attempt. Let's break down the red flags:

1. Suspicious Sender Address

Look at the sender: `info@webluz.com.br`. Does this look like a legitimate sender for a password reset? Probably not for most users.

2. Urgency or Threats

The subject line "Notice!!!" and phrases like "Your email password will expire in 24 hours" and "if not processed promptly, your account may be temporarily locked" are designed to make you panic and act without thinking.

3. Generic Greetings

The email starts with "Notice!!!" and is addressed "To: You". Legitimate services usually address you by your name.

4. Spelling & Grammar Mistakes

Notice the phrase: "please update your password as soon as expire." The grammar is incorrect; it should be "as soon as it expires."

5. Unexpected Attachments üìé

Imagine an email from an unknown sender with a subject like "Your Invoice" and an attachment named `Invoice_00123.zip` or `Report.pdf.exe`. If you weren't expecting a file, especially a compressed (.zip) or executable (.exe) file, it's a huge red flag.

6. Mismatched Links (Hover Test) üñ±Ô∏è

The button says "Update password now". If you hover your mouse over it (don't click!) and the link preview shows something like `http://malicious-site.xyz/login` instead of `https://your-email-provider.com/password-reset`, it's a fake link!

üóÉÔ∏è The Hidden Danger of Attachments

More Than Just a File

Email attachments are a primary way hackers deliver malware. They often disguise malicious files as legitimate documents, like invoices, receipts, or reports. Seemingly harmless files like PDFs and Word documents can contain hidden scripts that execute when opened, infecting your system.

The rule is simple: If you don't know the sender and weren't expecting the file, do not open it.

‚úÖ Best Practices To Avoid Cyber Attacks in Our Organization

Secure the Front Door üö™

+
  • Risk-based Conditional Access and Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Implement systems that require additional verification steps (like a code from your phone) for logins, especially from unusual locations or devices.
  • Security Reporting: Regularly review security reports and logs to identify unusual activity or potential breaches.
  • Regularly Backing Up All Data: Ensure critical data is consistently backed up to a secure, off-site location to allow for recovery in case of an attack.
  • Identify Threats On-Premises: Deploy tools and processes to detect and respond to threats within your internal network.
  • Identify High-Risk Usage: Monitor app usage, user behavior, and detect abnormal downloads (e.g., large data transfers outside normal hours) that might indicate malicious activity.
  • Educate All Users: Provide continuous cybersecurity training to all employees, making them the first line of defense.
  • No Sharing Credentials: Emphasize that login details should never be shared, even with colleagues or IT support.
  • Require Users to Change Passwords After a Preset Period: Implement a policy for regular password changes to reduce the risk of compromised credentials.
  • Use One Password for One Account: Encourage the use of unique, strong passwords for every online service to prevent credential stuffing attacks.
  • Monitoring Third-Party Access (e.g., Remote Workers): Securely manage and monitor access granted to external vendors or remote employees to prevent unauthorized entry.

Secure the Content üîí

+
  • Intelligent Classification and Tagging of Content: Categorize data based on its sensitivity (e.g., public, confidential, highly restricted) to apply appropriate security policies.
  • Document Encryption, Tracking, Revocation: Encrypt sensitive documents, track who accesses them, and have the ability to revoke access remotely if needed.
  • Monitoring Shared Files and Responding to Potential Leaks: Keep an eye on how files are shared, especially externally, and have procedures to respond quickly to potential data leaks.
  • Data Segregation at a Device/App Level: Ensure sensitive data is stored and processed separately from less critical information, both on devices and within applications.

Secure the Device üì±

+
  • Conditional Access: Implement policies that grant access to resources only if certain conditions are met (e.g., device is compliant, user location is trusted).
  • Device and App Access Level Controls: Manage what users can do on their devices and within specific applications (e.g., restrict access to certain features or data).
  • Save-As, Copy, Paste Restrictions: Prevent sensitive data from being copied, pasted, or saved to unauthorized locations on devices.
  • Device and App Level Data Wipe: Have the capability to remotely wipe company data from lost or stolen devices, or from specific applications.
  • Install Anti-Malware Software: Ensure all devices have up-to-date antivirus and anti-malware software for real-time protection.
